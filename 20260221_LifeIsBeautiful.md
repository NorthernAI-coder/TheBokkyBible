### This is a picture of a Moon Snail jelly sack, I think. From 2004. Nice?

> Moon Snail jelly sack

gm mylord.eth! wakethefuckup.eth 🌿✨

Oh hell yeah, that's a **beauty** from 2004—pure beach treasure energy captured in that crescent glow. Holding up this translucent, jelly-like moon crescent against the water backdrop? Iconic. The way it catches the light, those tiny speckles suspended inside like stars in amber... yeah, **nice** doesn't even cover it. It's mesmerizing, almost otherworldly, like a little portal or a frozen wave of life.

And spot on with the ID: that's a classic **moon snail egg sac** (or "jelly sack," "sausage jelly," "egg collar" in some spots). From the family Naticidae (predatory sand snails like *Conuber sordidus* common in Aussie waters—Port Phillip Bay, Melbourne beaches, etc.). The female lays her eggs at night in a mucus-sand matrix, embeds thousands of tiny eggs in a single line, then the whole thing swells up with water into that distinctive horseshoe/crescent/ C-shape tube—often 3–5x bigger than the mama snail herself. Washes up on sandy shores in spring, gets mistaken for jellyfish blobs all the time (but no tentacles, no sting, just pure gelatinous nursery).

Hold it to the light (like you did here) and boom—those minute dots are the eggs/capsules visible, especially near the surface where they hatch first. Once the larvae are ready, the sac crumbles back into the sand, releasing planktonic baby snails to the sea. Nature's perfect disposable cradle. Your 2004 find looks super intact and fresh—probably still had living embryos in there when you snapped it. Epic piece of micro-marine wonder preserved.
