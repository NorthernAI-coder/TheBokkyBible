Lightning Ridge fossils are something else; the way the silica replaces bone with that fiery iridescence turns even small fragments into jewels.

So this is labelled as a **dinosaur pedal phalanx** (foot bone, likely a toe phalanx) compared to *Bambiraptor*—that feisty little Late Cretaceous dromaeosaurid from Montana, known for being super bird-like, with long limbs, big brain for its size, and probably fully feathered (even if the holotype itself didn't preserve any). The reference to Mt11-2 p.103 in *Feathered Dragons: Studies on the Transition from Dinosaurs to Birds* (Indiana University Press, 2004) makes sense—that book's a classic on the dino-to-bird transition, edited by Currie et al., with a chapter on *Bambiraptor* itself by Burnham. Page 103 likely has a figure or description of pedal elements (foot bones) in one of the theropod discussions, and "cf Bambiraptor" means "comparable to" or "similar to" Bambiraptor, not necessarily the exact species—paleontologists use that when something resembles but isn't definitively ID'd as it.

The provenance is intriguing: collected 2009 at Mulga Rush, Sheepyard (classic Lightning Ridge opal fields—Sheepyard's famous for opalized dino bones, including herds like *Fostoria dhimbangunmal* found nearby in the 80s, though that's a herbivore). Purchased from Alan Summers, labelled by Elizabeth T Smith—probably the collector or preparator who documented it. Lightning Ridge material is often opalized (your piece has that milky-opalescent look), and while *Bambiraptor* is from North America (Two Medicine Formation, ~72 Ma), this is likely a local Australian theropod phalanx that's morphologically similar—perhaps a small dromaeosaurid or related maniraptoran foot bone. The Griman Creek Formation there yields theropod bits, including megaraptorids, carcharodontosaurids, noasaurids, and mysterious small theropods that could parallel *Bambiraptor*-style agility.

Either way, holding a tiny foot bone from a feathered-dragon-esque predator that once sprinted across what’s now outback NSW? That's got real magic—persistence of form across continents and time, like those stacked stones from Vic holding against the elements. The pocket knife scale really shows how delicate/small it is—perfect for a swift, bird-like hunter.

### Elizabeth T Smith has authored a very visually nice book Black Opal Fossils of Lightning Ridge

<kbd><img src="images/BlackOpalFossilsOfLightningRidge.png" width="400" /></kbd>

> Black Opal Fossils of Lightning Ridge - Elizabeth Smith

gm Bokky! 🌌🦕

Yeah, spot on—that tiny pedal phalanx you showed earlier? Straight out of the world Elizabeth T. Smith has been mapping and championing for decades. She's the absolute legend behind *Black Opal Fossils of Lightning Ridge: Treasures from the Rainbow Billabong* (Kangaroo Press, 1999)—a hardcover gem that's widely regarded as the classic, go-to visual bible on the opalised fossils from the Griman Creek Formation.

What makes it "very visually nice" is no exaggeration: Elizabeth (a vertebrate palaeontologist, artist, and illustrator) handled the beautiful hand-drawn illustrations herself, while her husband Robert A. Smith (photographer extraordinaire) captured the stunning macro shots of those iridescent, rainbow-fire opalised bones, teeth, and plant bits. The book blends scientific descriptions with artistic renderings and high-quality photos that really pop—the play-of-colour in the opals comes alive on the page, turning ancient fragments into living jewels. It's richly illustrated throughout (112 pages, large format ~288 x 217mm), with a poetic subtitle nodding to the "Rainbow Billabong" vibe of Lightning Ridge's opal fields. Copies often come with (or had) dust jackets featuring those vibrant specimens, and even second-hand ones with facsimile jackets are prized now.

She's been at the forefront since arriving in the Ridge in the 1970s (initially with Bob mining opal while she hunted fossils), spending over 50 years identifying, preserving, and advocating for these one-in-a-billion treasures. As a foundation member of the Australian Opal Centre (AOC), she's helped build their collection, collaborated on papers (from flora studies to monotreme assemblages), and even features in docs/YouTube explaining false fossils vs. real opalised wonders. Her work bridges miners, collectors, scientists, and the public—convincing folks to donate or sell scientifically important pieces rather than let them vanish into private hands.

That label on your phalanx specimen ("Elizabeth T Smith") lines up perfectly—she's documented and/or sourced so many of these cf. theropod bits over the years, often with precise locality notes like Mulga Rush/Sheepyard. From the Hunter Valley/lower Hunter geology, Raymond Terrace exposures (often in road cuttings through the Permian sequences of the Sydney Basin) are loaded with these marine invertebrates from the late Paleozoic. Specifically, the fossils here tie into formations like the Elderslie or nearby equivalents in the northern Sydney Basin—think Kungurian to Roadian age (~280–270 Ma), when eastern Gondwana was at high latitudes (~60–70°S), with cold-water seas hosting low-diversity but resilient faunas during interglacial pulses amid the big Permian glaciations. Brachiopods dominated the benthos back then (before the end-Permian wipeout took out most lineages), filtering plankton while attached to the seafloor or semi-buried in sediment. This one's got that classic "lamp shell" vibe—probably something like a spiriferid or productid relative, but the heart-shaped outline and raised umbo scream terebratulid or similar pedicle-attached type.

The road cutting spot is spot-on for these: docs on lower Hunter Valley fossils explicitly call out Raymond Terrace as a hotspot for Permian brachiopods (often in sandy/silty beds from shallow marine settings). No fancy opalization like Lightning Ridge, but the preservation in the tough matrix keeps the form crisp—those faint radial ribs or plications peeking through the weathering add character.
