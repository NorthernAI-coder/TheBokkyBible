From evolutionary psychology and social identity theory (Tajfel, Brewer, etc.), humans are primed for **in-group favoritism** — stronger cooperation, trust, and giving within the perceived "us." This isn't a bug; it's a feature from deep time.

- Small hunter-gatherer bands (~20-150 people) faced constant intergroup competition/threat. Favoring your coalition (parochial altruism) boosted survival odds: share food/resources freely inside → fight/hate harder outside.
- Oxytocin (bonding hormone) amps up for in-group → dulls empathy for out-group.
- Brain regions like medial prefrontal cortex light up more for "us" → less for "them."
- Result: giving flows easy when separation dissolves (group feels like extended self). Perceived isolation/out-group status triggers scarcity mindset — "fight for my slice" → grasping tightens.

Religions often amplify this: many major ones historically frame their adherents as the **in-group** ("chosen," "saved," "enlightened") and outsiders as threats/misguided/lost. This isn't universal across all traditions, but it's a recurring pattern — reinforces cohesion inside, justifies boundaries (or worse) outside. Evolutionary psych sees it as coalition-building tool scaled up: religion binds large groups via shared sacred narratives, rituals, moral codes → outcompetes looser tribes.

- Deep time biology/evolution screams **interconnectedness**. All humans trace back to common African ancestors ~200-300k years ago. We're one extended family tree — branches, not separate species.

- Ecology echoes it: no organism exists in isolation; we're all entangled in webs of reciprocity, symbiosis, predation.
- Zen/Buddhist remix: the "optical delusion of separation" (as Einstein phrased it in similar spirit) — everything interdependent, impermanent, flowing.

When religions teach this scale — that humanity is one interconnected stream through time, not silos of saved vs. damned — the perceived separation shrinks. In-group expands toward species-wide (or planetary). Giving without grasping becomes default: why grasp when there's no real "other" to lose to?
