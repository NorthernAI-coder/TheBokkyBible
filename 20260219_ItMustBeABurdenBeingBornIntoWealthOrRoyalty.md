And yeah — being born into wealth or royalty? It's a heavier burden than most outsiders ever clock.

On paper it looks like cheat codes: no survival stress, networks on tap, doors that open before you knock. But the trade-offs are brutal and subtle:

- **Identity gets hijacked early** — You're not really "you"; you're the heir, the name, the bloodline, the walking balance sheet. Every choice gets filtered through "what does this mean for The Family / The House / The Legacy?" → authenticity becomes a luxury you can't afford.

- **Trust is poisoned** — Almost impossible to know if someone loves *you* or the access / status / future payout you represent. Paranoia creeps in, or worse, you become the paranoid one doing the testing. Real intimacy? Rare as hell.

- **Pressure without outlet** — Expectations are galactic. Failures aren't just personal; they're dynastic scandals. The "golden cage" cliché is real: freedom of movement, but zero freedom to be mediocre, to experiment, to fall flat without it echoing through generations.

- **Spiritual atrophy risk** — When survival isn't on the table, the soul's muscle for grit, humility, surrender doesn't get the same workout. Some never develop it. Others overcompensate with extreme asceticism or philanthropy just to feel *something* real.

- **Empire-level attachment** — The whole system is built on clinging: to status, to control, to the myth of superiority. Letting go — the exact move the Bible's been chanting about in Books 2 and 3 — is heresy against the operating system you were born running.

Plenty of them wake up anyway. Some quietly subvert from the inside, others walk away entirely (the rare "renunciation" stories that actually stick). But the default path? It's a gilded burden that can crush the spirit faster than poverty ever could, because at least poverty forces you to confront illusion head-on.
