But it comes with this shadow: the helper fills the care gap so parents can work/white-collar grind, yet remains structurally undervalued. The disdain you saw in those children wasn't earned by merit; it was inherited privilege, amplified by cultural acceptance of the master-servant frame (sometimes dressed up as "like family" when convenient, but rarely truly equal).

Those "chaos monkeys" (love the term—perfect for the entitled, dependency-blind kids and adults running on autopilot privilege) have structured their entire lives around *not* doing the foundational labor that keeps everything humming. Cooking, cleaning, childcare, laundry, maintenance—the invisible infrastructure of daily life. In Malaysia (and much of Southeast Asia), wealthy and upper-middle households often lean heavily on migrant domestic workers (mostly women from Indonesia, Philippines, etc.) to outsource all that. Estimates put 300,000–400,000 domestic workers in the country, with one in five households employing at least one.

If those workers collectively walked away? The system would grind to a halt fast.

- **Immediate chaos in the household** — Meals don't get prepped, homes don't stay clean, kids don't get watched or shuttled, elderly care falters. Busy professionals (especially dual-income families) suddenly face the full load they offloaded. Many wouldn't know where to start—basic skills atrophied from disuse. Panic hiring attempts would spike, but with labor shortages already chronic (exacerbated by migration restrictions, pandemics, and poor treatment driving workers away), replacements wouldn't materialize overnight.

- **Economic ripple effects** — Parents miss work or cut hours to cover gaps → productivity dips. Businesses that rely on those professionals feel it. In extreme hypotheticals (like a mass walkout or strike), it could force negotiations or policy shifts—though history shows pushback is fierce (arrests, deportations, blame-shifting to "lazy" or "ungrateful" workers).

Real-world parallels exist: when domestic workers flee abusive setups en masse or when countries tighten inflows (e.g., Indonesia pausing exports to Malaysia over abuse concerns), households scramble.
